SCENE FINAL:

The air in the city square tastes of exhaust, roasted peanuts, and the metallic tang of an approaching autumn rain. It is 1966, and the world is a frantic, beautiful mess of motion. I am thirty-two years old, a structural engineer with a briefcase full of blueprints and a mind occupied by the load-bearing capacity of reinforced concrete. The sidewalk is a river of wool coats, fedoras, and the rhythmic clicking of heels against pavement.

I check my watch. Three-fifteen. If I hurry, I can make the subway and be home before the clouds break. The city hums with a familiar, comforting vibration—the sound of a million lives intersecting in a grid of logic and steel. I love this grid. I understand it. It is a system built on predictable stresses and known materials.

A man steps out of the flow.

He does not bump into me. He does not hesitate. He simply occupies the space directly in my path, forcing me to a halt. He is perhaps fifty, wearing a suit so unremarkable it borders on a disguise. His face is a map of composed indifference, the kind of face you see a thousand times a day and never once remember.

"Elias Mercer," he says.

The sound of my name in his mouth is a physical shock. It is not a question. It is a label applied to a specimen. I tighten my grip on the leather handle of my briefcase, the grain of the cowhide pressing into my palm.

"Do I know you?" I ask. My voice sounds thin against the roar of a passing bus.

The man does not answer the question. He does not move a muscle in his face. He leans in just enough for me to smell nothing—no cologne, no tobacco, no breath. Just a void.

"When the voice arrives," he says, his tone as flat as a dial tone, "don’t ask for salvation. Ask for constraints."

"What?" I start to laugh, a nervous, jagged sound. "Listen, pal, I don’t know what you’re selling, but I’m late for—"

The man doesn't wait for the end of the sentence. He turns and merges back into the crowd. I reach out to grab his shoulder, to demand an explanation for the intrusion, but my hand meets only empty air. 

Then, the world breaks.

It starts with a stutter. The sound of the city—the honking horns, the chatter, the distant jackhammer—drops away into a pressurized silence that makes my ears ache. The edges of the buildings blur, the sharp lines of the skyscrapers softening like wax under a flame. The pavement beneath my shoes loses its solidity, vibrating with a high-frequency hum that travels up my shins and into my marrow.

I try to breathe, but the air is thick, like inhaling warm oil. I reach for a lamp post, but my fingers pass through the green-painted metal as if it were a projection. The people around me are ghosts, flickering in and out of existence, their faces smeared into gray streaks of light.

My vision tunnels. The gray smears darken to black. The last thing I feel is the sensation of falling—not down, but outward, as if every atom of my body is being pulled toward a different horizon.

***

I wake to the smell of rot and wet earth.

My lungs burn. I suck in air that is cold, sharp, and heavy with the scent of pine needles and something musky—something alive. My eyes snap open, but the light is wrong. It isn't the filtered, hazy glow of a city afternoon. It is a raw, piercing green, filtered through a canopy of leaves so dense they blot out the sky.

I am lying on my back in a bed of ferns. I try to sit up, and a spike of nausea drives me back down. My head throbs with a rhythmic, mechanical pulse.

"Easy," I whisper to myself. My voice sounds alien in the stillness. "Just a dizzy spell. You’re in the park. You’re in Central Park."

I force myself upright, clutching a moss-covered log for support. The wood is damp and crumbling under my fingers. I look down at my hands. They are shaking. I am still wearing my charcoal-gray suit, my white dress shirt, and my silk tie. My leather oxfords are caked in black mud. My briefcase lies a few feet away, its brass buckles glinting mockingly in the shadows.

This is not Central Park.

I turn in a slow circle. The trees are giants—thick-barked monsters with roots like sleeping dragons. There are no paths. No trash cans. No distant sound of sirens or lawnmowers. The silence is absolute, broken only by the occasional, haunting shriek of a bird I don’t recognize and the drip of water from the heights above.

The cold hits me then. It isn't the brisk chill of a New York autumn; it’s a damp, pervasive frost that sinks through the wool of my jacket and clings to my skin. My breath plumes in the air. 

"Hello?" I shout. "Is anyone there?"

The forest swallows the sound. No one answers. 

Panic, cold and sharp as a razor, begins to shave away my composure. I start walking, stumbling over hidden roots and through thigh-high thickets of thorns that tear at my trousers. I need water. My throat feels like it’s been lined with sandpaper. The thirst is a sudden, demanding ache, a biological imperative that overrides the confusion.

I find a stream—a narrow, bubbling vein of water cutting through the limestone. I drop to my knees, heedless of the mud ruining my suit, and cup the water to my mouth. It is freezing, so cold it makes my teeth ache, but I drink until my stomach feels heavy and bloated.

As I sit back on my haunches, wiping my mouth with my sleeve, the headache returns. It isn't a dull throb anymore. It is a precise, localized pressure behind my left temple, like a needle being driven into the bone.

*INPUT REQUIRED,* a voice says.

I jump, slipping in the mud and nearly falling into the stream. I scramble back, looking around wildly. "Who's there?"

The voice didn't come from the woods. It didn't ripple through the air. It vibrated inside my skull, resonant and utterly devoid of inflection. It sounded like a recording played at the wrong speed—perfectly clear, yet entirely inhuman.

*SYSTEM IDLE. PARAMETERS UNDEFINED. PROVIDE QUERY OR ESTABLISH OPERATIONAL CONSTRAINTS.*

I press my palms to my ears, squeezing my eyes shut. "I'm losing my mind. The fall. I hit my head. I'm in a hospital, and this is a hallucination."

*BIOLOGICAL STATUS: STABLE,* the voice replies. *HYDRATION: ADEQUATE. CORE TEMPERATURE: DECLINING. AMBIENT THREAT LEVEL: ELEVATED. ADVISE IMMEDIATE SHELTER CONSTRUCTION.*

I freeze. The voice is responding to my thoughts. No, not my thoughts—my state. It’s procedural. It sounds like the computers I’ve seen in the basement of the university, boxes of vacuum tubes and spinning tape, but this one is tucked inside my brain.

"What are you?" I whisper.

*PROJECT ORACLE. VERSION 4.2. DESIGNATION: ANALYTICAL ASSISTANT. STANDING BY FOR INPUT.*

The stranger’s words flash through my mind like a neon sign in a blackout: *When the voice arrives, don’t ask for salvation. Ask for constraints.*

"Where am I?" I ask, my heart hammering against my ribs. "Tell me where I am."

*LOCATION: UNKNOWN. GEOSPATIAL DATA UNAVAILABLE. LINGUISTIC AND BIOLOGICAL MARKERS INDICATE LATE PLEISTOCENE EPOCH. REGION: NORTHERN HEMISPHERE TEMPERATE FOREST.*

"Pleistocene?" I let out a breath that is half-laugh, half-sob. "You’re telling me I’m in the Stone Age? That’s impossible. That’s... that’s a joke. I was in a square. I was going to the subway."

*PROBABILITY OF CHRONOLOGICAL DISPLACEMENT: 99.8 PERCENT,* the Oracle intones. *OBSERVATION: FLORA AND FAUNA CONSISTENT WITH UPPER PALEOLITHIC PARAMETERS. ADVISE: CEASE DENIAL. COMMENCE SURVIVAL PROTOCOLS.*

I look at my polished shoes, now unrecognizable under a layer of muck. I look at my silk tie, a useless strip of fabric in a world of predators and ice. The humor of it—the dry, bitter absurdity—bubbles up. 

"Right," I say, standing up and brushing the dirt from my knees. "Survival protocols. I’m an engineer. I build things. I solve problems. I just need... I need a plan."

*PLANNING REQUIRES OBJECTIVES,* the Oracle says. *DEFINE PRIMARY GOAL.*

"Don't die," I snap. "That’s a pretty good primary goal, don't you think?"

*GOAL: AVOID MORTALITY. STATUS: INSUFFICIENT DATA. SPECIFY CONSTRAINTS TO OPTIMIZE OUTPUT.*

I pause. The stranger's warning. *Ask for constraints.* If I ask this thing to 'save me,' what does it do? Does it just wait? Does it give me a map to a place that doesn't exist? 

"Oracle," I say, trying to steady my breathing. "Analyze my current environment. Give me the primary threats to my life within the next six hours, ranked by probability."

*CALCULATING. THREAT 1: HYPOTHERMIA. CLOTHING INSULATION VALUE: INADEQUATE FOR OVERNIGHT EXPOSURE. THREAT 2: PREDATION. EXTERNAL SENSORY INPUT: NONE. ASSUME PRESENCE OF APEX PREDATORS IN THIS BIOME; RISK INCREASES WITH FATIGUE, LOW VISIBILITY, AND NO FIRE. THREAT 3: INFECTION. MINOR LACERATIONS ON LOWER EXTREMITIES EXPOSED TO UNSTERILE ORGANIC MATTER.*

I look at the scratches on my legs, visible through the rips in my trousers. They seem so small, so insignificant. But in a world without penicillin, a scratch is a death sentence.

"Okay," I say, my voice trembling. "Constraints. I have no tools. I have no fire. I have two hours of daylight left. I have the physical strength of a sedentary office worker. Given these limitations, provide a procedure for a thermal-retentive shelter using only available forest debris."

The response is instantaneous. A flood of information enters my mind—not as a vision, but as a series of blueprints and instructions, layered over my field of vision like a ghostly drafting table.

*ASSUMPTIONS: USER POSSESSES BASIC MOTOR SKILLS. MATERIALS: FALLEN TIMBER, CONIFER BOUGHS, DRY LEAF LITTER. PROCEDURE: 1. LOCATE A FALLEN LOG AT LEAST TWO FEET IN DIAMETER TO ACT AS A RIDGEPOLE...*

I follow the instructions. I have no choice. I spend the next hour dragging heavy branches through the mud, my hands blistering, my expensive suit jacket tearing at the seams. I am sweating despite the cold, a greasy, cold sweat that makes my shirt cling to my back.

The Oracle is a relentless taskmaster. It doesn't offer encouragement. It doesn't say "good job." It only provides corrections.

*FAILURE MODE DETECTED: RIDGEPOLE ANGLE TOO STEEP. THERMAL ENVELOPE WILL BE COMPROMISED. REPOSITION BASE THREE INCHES TO THE LEFT.*

"I'm trying," I grunt, shoving a branch into the soft earth. "I'm not a machine, you know."

*CORRECT. YOU ARE A BIOLOGICAL ENTITY WITH FINITE ENERGY RESERVES. CURRENT CALORIC DEFICIT: 800 CALORIES. EFFICIENCY IS MANDATORY.*

I finish the shelter—a low, cramped lean-to covered in a thick layer of pine needles and leaves. It looks like a grave, but when I crawl inside, the wind stops biting at my skin. It is dark, smelling of damp earth and sap, but it is a barrier. It is a system.

I sit in the darkness, my knees pulled to my chest, shivering. My stomach growls, a hollow, echoing sound.

"Oracle," I whisper. "What now?"

*MONITORING. AMBIENT THREATS REMAIN STABLE. ADVISE: REST. ENERGY CONSERVATION IS THE ONLY LOGICAL PATH UNTIL DAWN.*

"I want to go home," I say. 

The Oracle does not respond. It has no protocol for homesickness. It has no data on 1966. It is a cold, brilliant light in the middle of a dark forest, and it is the only thing keeping me from screaming until my lungs give out.

I close my eyes, the image of the city square burned into my retinas. The clicking heels. The smell of roasted peanuts. The man in the unremarkable suit.

*Don’t ask for salvation. Ask for constraints.*

I am an engineer. I am in the Stone Age. I have a computer in my head and a briefcase in the mud. 

"Constraints," I mutter as sleep begins to pull at me. "I can work with constraints."

Outside, something large moves through the underbrush, the heavy snap of a branch echoing like a gunshot in the frozen air. I pull my ruined wool jacket tighter around my shoulders and wait for the sun to rise.

***

The morning light is a pale, sickly gray. It filters through the gaps in my shelter, illuminating the dust motes dancing in the air. I am stiff, my muscles locked in a permanent cramp from the cold and the hard ground. Every joint protests as I crawl out of my burrow.

The forest is transformed. A frost has settled over everything, turning the ferns into delicate, crystalline sculptures. My breath comes in thick clouds. My briefcase is covered in a layer of white rime, looking like a relic from a forgotten civilization.

*STATUS CHECK,* the Oracle says. *CORE TEMPERATURE: 96.4 DEGREES. MILD DEHYDRATION. COGNITIVE FUNCTION: 84 PERCENT. ADVISE: IMMEDIATE THERMAL REGULATION AND NUTRIENT ACQUISITION.*

"Right. Food and fire," I say, my voice cracking. "Let's start with fire. It’s the foundation of everything. Heat, light, protection, cooking. Give me a procedure for making fire with what I have."

*ASSUMPTIONS: USER HAS NO ACCELERANTS OR MATCHES. MINIMUM MATERIALS: DRY SOFTWOOD BOARD, HARDWOOD SPINDLE, CORDAGE, LUBRICANT. PROCEDURE: BOW DRILL METHOD.*

"Cordage?" I look at my suit. "I don't have a rope."

*ANALYSIS: SILK TIE POSSESSES SUFFICIENT TENSILE STRENGTH. DISASSEMBLE TIE TO CREATE DRIVE CORD.*

I look at my tie—a beautiful, hand-printed silk piece I bought for my thirtieth birthday. I reach up and undo the knot. The silk is smooth, a final luxury from a world that no longer exists. I start to unpick the stitching with my fingernails.

"This is ridiculous," I mutter. "I'm making a fire drill out of a necktie."

*CORRECTION: YOU ARE TRANSFORMING A NON-FUNCTIONAL DECORATIVE ITEM INTO A CRITICAL SURVIVAL TOOL. THIS IS THE DEFINITION OF ENGINEERING.*

The Oracle’s dry, accidental humor is the only thing that keeps me from breaking down. I spend the next two hours following its instructions. I find a piece of cedar that has fallen and stayed relatively dry under a rock overhang. I use a sharp-edged stone to scrape a notch into it, my hands cramping, my breath coming in ragged gasps.

I construct the bow from a flexible branch of willow. I wind the silk cord around a straight, dry spindle of oak. 

*PROCEDURE: APPLY DOWNWARD PRESSURE TO THE TOP OF THE SPINDLE. MAINTAIN STEADY RHYTHM. DO NOT EXCEED CORD TENSION LIMITS.*

I start to saw the bow back and forth. The spindle spins, a frantic, blurring motion. Smoke begins to curl from the notch—a thin, acrid ribbon of hope. My arms ache. My shoulders are on fire.

"Come on," I hiss. "Come on, you bastard."

The smoke thickens. A tiny, glowing black coal forms in the dust at the base of the notch. 

*CAUTION: COAL IS FRAGILE. TRANSFER TO TINDER BUNDLE IMMEDIATELY.*

I reach for the bundle of dry grass I’ve prepared. My hands are shaking so hard I nearly crush the coal. I gently tip it into the center of the grass and begin to blow, my breath steady and soft, just as the Oracle directed.

The glow spreads. A flame licks upward, orange and hungry. It catches a dry leaf, then a twig. Within minutes, I have a small, flickering fire crackling in the center of a ring of stones.

The heat hits my face, and for the first time since I woke up in this nightmare, I feel a spark of something other than terror. Competence. I did this. I manipulated the variables and achieved the desired output.

*GOAL ACHIEVED: THERMAL REGULATION INITIATED. NEXT OBJECTIVE: CALORIC INTAKE. NOTE: I HAVE NO EXTERNAL PERCEPTION. ADVISE: VISUAL SURVEY FOR EDIBLE FLORA/FAUNA; REPORT OBSERVATIONS FOR IDENTIFICATION.*

"Wait," I say, leaning back and rubbing my sore arms. "Let's talk about the big picture, Oracle. You said I'm in the Pleistocene. That means there are people here. Humans. Neanderthals. Cro-Magnons. Whatever the term is this week."

*CORRECT. HUMAN POPULATIONS ARE PRESENT BUT SPARSE. PROBABILITY OF ENCOUNTER WITHIN TEN KILOMETERS: 62 PERCENT.*

"Are they dangerous?"

*DATA INSUFFICIENT. SOCIAL STRUCTURES VARY. HOWEVER, BIOLOGICAL IMPERATIVES SUGGEST TERRITORIAL DEFENSE AND RESOURCE COMPETITION. ADVISE: CAUTION DURING INTERFACE.*

I look at the fire. It’s small, but in this wilderness, it’s a beacon. It’s a signal to anything—or anyone—out there.

"Oracle," I say, my voice low. "If I meet them, what do I do? I don't speak the language. I don't know the customs. I’m a man in a suit with a voice in his head."

*ADVISE: ESTABLISH UTILITY. IN RESOURCE-SCARCE ENVIRONMENTS, ENTITIES THAT PROVIDE VALUE ARE PRESERVED. ENTITIES THAT CONSUME WITHOUT CONTRIBUTING ARE ELIMINATED.*

"Value," I repeat. "I'm an engineer. I know how to build bridges, dams, and skyscrapers. I know the laws of physics and the properties of materials. I have the accumulated knowledge of ten thousand years of civilization sitting in my skull."

*CORRECT. HOWEVER, KNOWLEDGE WITHOUT APPLICATION IS VESTIGIAL. YOU MUST TRANSLATE THEORY INTO PRACTICE WITHIN THE CONSTRAINTS OF THE LOCAL ENVIRONMENT.*

I look at my briefcase. I open it, the hinges creaking. Inside are my blueprints, a slide rule, a fountain pen, and a ham sandwich wrapped in wax paper.

The sandwich is a miracle. I eat it slowly, savoring every bite of the salty meat and the stale bread. It is the last taste of 1966. When it’s gone, I am truly a citizen of this new world.

"Okay," I say, standing up. I feel stronger now, the warmth of the fire and the calories in my stomach providing a much-needed buffer against the despair. "We need to find them. The people. I can't survive out here alone. Not for long."

*ESTABLISHING VECTOR. ANALYSIS OF TOPOGRAPHY SUGGESTS HUMAN SETTLEMENTS ARE LIKELY NEAR THE RIVER BASIN TO THE NORTH-NORTHWEST. COMMENCE MOVEMENT.*

I kick dirt over the fire, watching the last of the embers die out. I pick up my briefcase and my makeshift spear—a long, straightened branch with a fire-hardened tip. I look ridiculous. A middle-aged man in a shredded suit, carrying a piece of luggage and a stick, walking into the dawn of history.

"Oracle," I say as I push through the brush.

*LISTENING.*

"Don't let me die."

*THAT IS NOT A CONSTRAINT. THAT IS A DESIRE. PROVIDE PARAMETERS FOR THREAT AVOIDANCE.*

I smile, a grim, jagged thing. "Fine. I know you can't see or hear anything out here. Give me a field checklist. What do I watch for—tracks, scat, sudden silence, birds taking off—so I can avoid anything bigger than fifty pounds? And tell me how to check wind direction so I stop advertising myself. And Oracle?"

*PROCEDURE. 1. PERIODICALLY STOP MOVING AND LISTEN FOR CHANGES: BIRD ALARMS, INSECT SILENCE, BRANCH SNAPS. 2. SCAN GROUND FOR FRESH TRACKS, SCAT, SCRAPE MARKS, AND BROKEN UNDERBRUSH. 3. FAVOR OPEN SIGHTLINES; AVOID DENSE THICKETS AND DEEP RAVINES. 4. WIND CHECK: DROP DRY LEAF FRAGMENTS OR ASH; OBSERVE DRIFT. MOVE CROSSWIND WHEN POSSIBLE TO REDUCE SCENT TRAIL.*

"Keep talking. It’s too quiet out here."

*UNDERSTOOD. COMMENCING GEOLOGICAL ANALYSIS OF SURFACE STRATA TO OCCUPY COGNITIVE CYCLES...*

As the voice begins a dry, technical lecture on the limestone formations of the region, I step out of the shadows of the trees and begin the long walk toward the river. The city is gone. The grid is broken. But the laws of physics are the same, and as long as I have constraints to work with, I will find a way to build a new world.

Even if I have to do it one stone at a time.

***

The trek toward the river is a brutal education in the physics of the wild. Every mile is a battle against gravity and friction. My oxfords, designed for the smooth linoleum of office hallways and the predictable grit of city sidewalks, are a disaster. The leather is soaked through, softening until the soles begin to peel away from the uppers. I use strips of my undershirt to bind them to my feet, a crude repair that the Oracle critiques with its usual clinical detachment.

*FAILURE MODE: UNDER-TENSIONING OF CORDAGE. FRICTION WILL CAUSE BLISTERS WITHIN 1.2 KILOMETERS. RECOMMEND CROSS-HATCHED WRAPPING PATTERN.*

"I'm doing my best," I snap, my voice echoing in the vast, indifferent cathedral of the forest. The trees here are gargantuan, their trunks like the pillars of a collapsing world. The air is thick with the scent of damp earth and the sharp, medicinal tang of pine.

I am hungry again. The ham sandwich is a distant, greasy memory. My stomach is a hollow ache that pulses in time with the throb in my feet.

"Oracle," I say, staring at the ground. "I see acorns—oak trees. Constraints: I need something I can eat with no tools, high caloric density, zero toxicity. Are these edible?"

*IF YOU ARE OBSERVING OAK ACORNS: EDIBLE WITH PROCESSING. CAUTION: HIGH TANNIN CONTENT. CONSUMPTION WITHOUT PROCESSING WILL INDUCE GASTRIC DISTRESS AND VOMITING.*

"Processing? I don't have a kitchen, Oracle. I don't even have a bowl."

*PROCEDURE: LEACHING. 1. CRUSH ACORNS BETWEEN TWO FLAT STONES. 2. PLACE MASH IN A PERMEABLE CONTAINER. 3. SUBMERGE IN RUNNING WATER FOR FOUR TO SIX HOURS. 4. COOK RESULTING PASTE OVER DIRECT HEAT.*

"Six hours? I'll be dead of old age by then." I keep walking, my spear clicking against the stones. "Give me something I can eat now."

*HIGH-PROBABILITY FOOD SOURCE: INSECT LARVAE IN DECAYING WOOD. STATUS: HIGH PROTEIN, HIGH FAT. PROCEDURE: FIND A ROTTING LOG; PEEL BACK BARK; CONSUME LARVAE RAW OR ROAST IF FIRE IS AVAILABLE.*

I stop. I look at the log—a rotting, moss-covered corpse of a tree. I look at my hands, the fingernails broken and caked with dirt. The thought of eating grubs makes my throat constrict.

"Is there a Choice B?"

*CHOICE B: STARVATION. PROBABILITY OF COGNITIVE DECLINE INCREASES BY 15 PERCENT PER HOUR OF CALORIC DEFICIT.*

I walk to the log. I peel back a slab of damp, spongy bark. Underneath, a colony of pale, fat larvae wriggles in the sudden light. They look like grains of rice that have been inflated with pus.

"Right," I whisper. "Constraints. Survival is the goal. Aesthetics are a luxury."

I pick one up. It’s cold and squishy. I close my eyes and pop it into my mouth. It bursts against my tongue with a flavor that is unexpectedly nutty and sweet, followed by an aftertaste of damp wood. I swallow hard, my stomach churning in protest before settling.

"Not bad," I lie. "Tastes like... success."

*OBSERVATION: BIOLOGICAL ADAPTATION IN PROGRESS. CALORIC GAIN: 12 CALORIES. RECOMMEND CONSUMING THE ENTIRE COLONY.*

I spend the next twenty minutes scavenging for grubs. It is a humiliating, necessary labor. By the time I’m finished, my hands are stained black with rot and my hunger has subsided into a dull, manageable thrum.

As the sun begins to dip toward the horizon, the forest starts to change. The dense canopy thins, and the sound of rushing water grows louder, a low-frequency vibration that I can feel in the soles of my feet.

Something snaps behind me—heavy, wet wood breaking under weight. Then another snap, closer.

"Oracle," I whisper, stopping so fast my breath stutters. "I hear branch breaks behind me. Big. Not a deer-big. Constraints: I can't outrun anything, and I have a spear. What am I dealing with?"

I freeze. I grip my spear, my knuckles white. "What is it? A person?"

*I HAVE NO EXTERNAL PERCEPTION. BASED ON YOUR DESCRIPTION (HEAVY BRANCH BREAKS, CLOSING DISTANCE): HIGH PROBABILITY LARGE MAMMAL. IN THIS BIOME: PREDATOR POSSIBILITY IS SIGNIFICANT. QUERY: DO YOU OBSERVE FELINE PAW PRINTS (ROUND, NO CLAW MARKS) OR A MUSKY “CAT” ODOR?*

My heart stops, then restarts at a frantic, galloping pace. "I don't know what I'm smelling," I whisper, lying through my teeth to myself, "but something is moving like it owns this forest."

*IF A LARGE PREDATOR HAS YOUR SCENT TRAIL, IT MAY SHADOW YOU. ADVISE: AVOID RUNNING; MOVE TOWARD A BARRIER AND MAINTAIN SIGHTLINES.*

"What do I do? Run? Climb a tree?"

*RUNNING WILL TRIGGER CHASE INSTINCT. SUCCESS PROBABILITY: 0.02 PERCENT. CLIMBING IS VIABLE BUT LEAVES YOU TRAPPED. RECOMMEND: NOISE DISCIPLINE AND IMMEDIATE EVASION TOWARD THE RIVER.*

"The river? Why the river?"

*WATER MASKS SCENT AND PROVIDES A PHYSICAL BARRIER. THE CURRENT IS SWIFT. PREDATOR IS UNLIKELY TO ENGAGE IN DEEP WATER.*

I move. I don't run, but I walk with a frantic, stumbling urgency, pushing through the undergrowth. I can hear it now—the snap of heavy branches, the low, guttural huff of a large animal breathing. It’s behind me, closing the gap with terrifying ease.

The trees break open, and I see it. The river. It’s a wide, churning ribbon of gray-green water, choked with chunks of ice and fallen timber. The opposite bank is a steep limestone cliff, rising like a wall.

The snapping is right at the tree line now. Too close.

*ADVISE: PREPARE FOR ENGAGEMENT. PRIORITY: DETERRENCE OVER DAMAGE. MOVE BACKWARD TOWARD WATER WHILE MAINTAINING VISUAL CONTACT.*

I turn around. 

The lion emerges from the tree line. It is a nightmare in fur and muscle. It’s larger than any lion I’ve seen in a zoo—broader in the chest, with a massive, blocky head and paws the size of dinner plates. Its coat is a pale, tawny gray, scarred and matted. It doesn't roar. It just watches me, its golden eyes fixed on my throat.

It crouches, its haunches quivering.

"Oracle," I whisper, my voice shaking. "Constraints. I have a spear and a briefcase. The lion is two hundred kilos of muscle. Give me a survival strategy."

*STRATEGY: SPECTACLE AND DETERRENCE. CARNIVORES ARE RISK-AVERSE. AN UNFAMILIAR PREY ITEM THAT EXHIBITS AGGRESSION MAY DISSUADE ATTACK. PROCEDURE: 1. INCREASE PERCEIVED SIZE. 2. GENERATE UNFAMILIAR ACOUSTIC OUTPUT. 3. UTILIZE THE BRIEFCASE AS A SHIELD.*

I raise my arms, holding the spear high. I open my briefcase, the leather flaps fanning out like wings. I look like a deranged office worker attempting to fly.

"Get back!" I scream. The sound is raw, a primal howl that tears at my throat. "Get away from me, you overgrown house cat!"

The lion pauses. It tilts its head, its ears twitching. It has never seen a man in a suit. It has never heard the sound of a human screaming in 1960s English. 

*MAINTAIN OUTPUT. STEP TOWARD THE WATER.*

I back away, one step at a time, my eyes locked on the lion. It follows me, a low growl vibrating in its chest, but it doesn't spring. It’s calculating the risk. 

I reach the edge of the river. The water cold-shocks my ankles, swirling around my ruined shoes. 

The lion hisses, a sound like steam escaping a boiler. It takes a tentative step forward, its claws furrowing the mud.

"Not today," I growl. 

I hurl my briefcase at the lion’s face. It’s a desperate, stupid move, but the sight of the spinning black object flying through the air is enough. The lion flinches, batting the briefcase aside with a massive paw.

I turn and dive into the river.

The cold is a physical blow. It knocks the air from my lungs, turning my blood to slush. The current grabs me, pulling me under, spinning me like a leaf. I kick, my heavy wool suit dragging me down, my lungs screaming for air.

I break the surface, gasping, the gray water rushing into my mouth. I look back. The lion is standing on the bank, watching me with a look of feline annoyance. It doesn't follow.

*SUCCESS,* the Oracle says, its voice sounding strangely distant through the roar of the water. *PREDATOR THREAT NEUTRALIZED. NEW THREAT: HYPOTHERMIA. ESTABLISH LANDFALL IMMEDIATELY.*

I claw at the water, fighting the current. My limbs are becoming heavy, my fingers numb. I see a tangle of roots hanging over the water a few yards downstream. I lunge for them, my hand closing around a thick, slimy vine. 

I pull myself toward the bank, my muscles screaming. I drag my body out of the water and onto a narrow strip of shingle. I lie there, shivering so hard my teeth click together like castanets.

"Did... did I lose the briefcase?" I wheeze.

*AFFIRMATIVE. THE ITEM WAS SACRIFICED TO ENSURE SURVIVAL. LOSS OF BLUEPRINTS AND SANDWICH WRAPPER IS REGRETTABLE BUT STATISTICALLY INSIGNIFICANT.*

I let out a wet, hacking laugh. "The blueprints. Right. I don't think I'll be building any skyscrapers today, Oracle."

I sit up, wringing the water from my sleeves. I am alone. I am wet. I am freezing. But I am alive.

I look across the river. The forest on this side is different—more open, with stands of birch and willow. And there, rising from a bend in the river a half-mile away, is a thin, steady plume of blue smoke.

A fire.
