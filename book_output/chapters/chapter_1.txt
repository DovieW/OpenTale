SCENE FINAL:

The air in the city square smells of roasted coffee, exhaust fumes, and the damp grit of a Chicago morning in late October. It is 1966, a year of steel and noise, and I am a man of schedules. I check my watch—a sturdy Bulova—and adjust the brim of my hat against a sudden gust of wind that swirls discarded newspapers around my ankles. The headlines are full of Vietnam and the space race, but my mind is occupied by the structural integrity of a new cantilevered bridge design on my desk at the firm.

I step off the curb, my brogues clicking against the pavement. The square is a hive of purposeful motion. Commuters in trench coats blur past me like ghosts in a long exposure photograph. The world is solid. It is dependable. It follows the laws of physics I spent four years mastering at university.

A man steps into my path.

He isn't running. He isn't rushing. He simply occupies the space where I am about to be, forcing me to a halt. He wears a charcoal suit that looks expensive but unremarkable, and his face is the kind of face you forget even while you are looking at it—composed, symmetrical, and entirely devoid of urgency.

"Elias Mercer," he says.

I stop. My heart gives a small, caffeine-fueled kick against my ribs. I don't recognize him, yet he says my name with the flat certainty of a banker reading a ledger. "Do I know you?"

The man doesn't answer the question. He doesn't smile. He leans in just enough for me to smell something sharp and metallic on his breath, like ozone or a soldering iron.

"When the voice arrives," he says, his voice cutting through the roar of city traffic with impossible clarity, "don’t ask for salvation. Ask for constraints."

"I'm sorry?" I blink, my hand tightening on the handle of my briefcase. "What voice? What are you—"

The man doesn't move, but the world around him begins to fail.

It starts with the sound. The screech of a bus’s brakes stretches out, deepening into a low, tectonic groan before vanishing into a vacuum. The chatter of the crowd is sucked away. Then the light goes wrong. The vibrant reds of a woman’s coat and the yellow of a passing taxi bleed into a muddy, flickering grey. The edges of the buildings blur, vibrating like a film reel melting in a projector.

I reach out to steady myself against a lamp post, but my hand passes through the cold metal as if it were smoke. Panic, hot and oily, rises in my throat.

"Wait," I try to shout, but I can't feel my tongue. I can't feel my lungs.

The stranger remains perfectly sharp, a high-definition figure in a dissolving world. He watches me with a clinical sort of curiosity as I collapse. Or rather, I don't collapse; I fall into a hole that has opened up in the very fabric of the afternoon. The pavement is gone. The sky is gone. There is only a crushing, silent weight, and then, nothing at all.

* * *

I wake up to the sound of something screaming.

It isn't a human scream. It’s a rhythmic, harsh screech that repeats three times before fading into the rustle of leaves. My eyes snap open, but the transition is so violent that my vision remains a smear of green and brown. I am lying on my back. Something cold and wet is seeping through the wool of my suit jacket.

I roll onto my side and retch, my stomach squeezing nothing but bile onto a bed of rotted pine needles. My head throbs with a rhythmic, stabbing pain behind my left eye, synchronized with the beat of my heart.

"Easy," I whisper to myself. The sound of my own voice is terrifyingly small. "Just… easy, Elias."

I sit up, and the world tilts. I am not in Chicago. I am not in 1966.

I am sitting in the middle of a forest so dense and ancient that the canopy above blots out the sun, leaving only a dim, cathedral light. The trees are giants—thick-barked monsters draped in moss the color of a bruise. The air is heavy, smelling of damp earth, decay, and a sharp, resinous scent I can't identify. It is cold. Not the bracing chill of a city autumn, but a deep, humid cold that settles into the marrow.

I look down at myself. I am still wearing my grey wool suit, my white button-down shirt, and my silk tie. My brogues are caked in mud. My briefcase is gone. My hat is gone. I look like a stray piece of litter dropped into a prehistoric museum.

"Hello?" I call out.

The forest swallows the word. No echo. No response. Just the drip-drip-drip of moisture falling from high branches.

I try to stand, but my knees are water. I manage to crawl to the base of a massive tree and lean my back against it. My breath comes in ragged, shallow gasps. I need to think. I am a structural engineer. I solve problems by breaking them down into their component parts.

Problem one: Location. Unknown. This isn't Lincoln Park. Problem secondary: Physical state. Nauseated, dizzy, likely concussed. Problem tertiary: Environment. Hostile.

I check my watch. The glass is cracked, and the hands are frozen at 10:14. The exact moment I stepped off the curb.

The cold is already starting to bite. My suit is designed for an office, not an overnight stay in a rainforest. My feet are numb. I need to move. I need to find a road, a telephone, a person.

I struggle to my feet, using the tree trunk for leverage. As I stand, a sound erupts inside my head. It isn't a sound I hear with my ears; it’s a vibration that starts at the base of my skull and radiates forward, settling behind my eyes. It feels like a migraine, but it has a rhythm. It has a structure.

*PROJECT ORACLE // TRANSFER EVENT: COMPLETE // SUBJECT: MERCER, ELIAS.*

I freeze. My hands fly to my ears, but the voice is inside. It is flat, sexless, and utterly devoid of inflection. It sounds like the recorded announcements at a train station, but stripped of any human warmth.

"Who's there?" I shout, spinning around. I lose my balance and stumble into a thicket of ferns. "Who is that?"

*INPUT REQUIRED. DEFINE PARAMETERS FOR INITIALIZATION.*

I scramble backward, my heart hammering against my ribs like a trapped bird. "I'm losing my mind. The fall… I hit my head. I'm having a stroke."

*NEGATIVE. NEUROLOGICAL INTEGRITY WITHIN ACCEPTABLE MARGINS. INITIALIZATION PENDING. PROVIDE GOAL OPTIMIZATION REQUEST.*

I squeeze my eyes shut and press my palms against my temples. "Stop it. Just stop it."

The voice doesn't stop. It doesn't even pause. It waits. It is a vacuum of a sound, an absence of humanity that demands to be filled. I remember the stranger in the square. *When the voice arrives, don’t ask for salvation. Ask for constraints.*

My breath hitched. "Salvation," I whisper. "Help me. Get me out of here. Take me home."

The silence that follows is worse than the voice. It lasts for ten seconds, then twenty. I think perhaps the hallucination has ended, but then the vibration returns, sharper this time.

*ERROR. 'SALVATION' IS NOT A DEFINED TECHNICAL PARAMETER. 'HOME' LACKS GEOSPATIAL COORDINATES. REQUESTS ARE VAGUE. PROVIDE QUANTIFIABLE CONSTRAINTS OR OBJECTIVES.*

"I don't understand," I moan, sliding down the trunk of the tree until I am huddled in a ball. "I'm cold. I'm thirsty. I don't know where I am."

*OBSERVATION: SUBJECT IS EXPERIENCING EARLY-STAGE HYPOTHERMIA AND MILD DEHYDRATION. AMBIENT TEMPERATURE IS DROPPING. PROBABILITY OF SYSTEMIC FAILURE WITHIN SIX HOURS: 74%.*

The clinical tone of the voice—the Oracle, I realize—sends a fresh jolt of terror through me. It isn't a rescuer. It isn't a guardian angel. It’s a calculator. It’s a goddamn slide rule in my head that is currently calculating the exact moment I will turn into a corpse.

"You can't help me?" I ask, my voice cracking.

*I PROVIDE KNOWLEDGE. I DO NOT PROVIDE MATERIALS. I DO NOT POSSESS PHYSICAL AGENCY. DEFINE CONSTRAINTS TO OPTIMIZE SURVIVAL.*

I take a ragged breath, trying to force my engineering brain to override my panicked monkey brain. *Ask for constraints.* The stranger had been specific. He hadn't said 'Ask for the answer.' He’d said 'Ask for constraints.'

"What… what are my constraints?" I ask.

*QUERY AMBIGUOUS. ASSUMING REQUEST FOR OPERATIONAL LIMITATIONS.* *LIMITATION 1: NO EXTERNAL PERCEPTION. I CANNOT SEE, HEAR, OR SMELL THE ENVIRONMENT. I RELY ENTIRELY ON SUBJECT DESCRIPTION.* *LIMITATION 2: NO MORALITY. I OPTIMIZE FOR THE STATED GOAL REGARDLESS OF ETHICAL COST UNLESS CONSTRAINED.* *LIMITATION 3: NO PHYSICAL MANIFESTATION. I CANNOT ALTER MATTER.* *LIMITATION 4: BANDWIDTH IS CONTINGENT ON SUBJECT STRESS LEVELS. HIGH PANIC DEGRADES DATA TRANSMISSION.*

I let out a hysterical, wet laugh. "So you're a blind, amoral encyclopedia stuck in my head?"

*ACCURATE SUMMARY,* the voice replies. *OBJECTIVE: SURVIVAL. CURRENT THREAT: EXPOSURE. DEFINE IMMEDIATE ACTIONS.*

I look at my trembling hands. The fingernails are already turning a faint shade of blue. The forest is darkening as the sun—wherever it is—sinks lower. I am a man who can design a bridge that holds ten thousand tons of steel, but I don't know how to survive a night in the woods with nothing but a wool suit and a broken watch.

"Okay," I whisper, closing my eyes to focus on the voice. "Objective: Establish shelter and warmth. Constraint: Use only what is within ten feet of me. Procedure?"

*ASSUMPTIONS: SUBJECT HAS NO TOOLS. SUBJECT HAS LIMITED CALORIC RESERVES. AMBIENT MOISTURE IS HIGH.* *MINIMUM MATERIALS: FALLEN BRANCHES, DRY LEAVES, SOFT EARTH.* *PROCEDURE: LOCATE A DEPRESSION OR THE LEE SIDE OF A LARGE TREE. CONSTRUCT A DEBRIS HUT. GATHER LARGE BRANCHES FOR A RIB-FRAME. LAYER SMALLER TWIGS. COVER WITH A MINIMUM OF TWO FEET OF COMPRESSED DRY LEAF LITTER FOR INSULATION.* *FAILURE MODES: INSUFFICIENT INSULATION THICKNESS LEADS TO RADIATIVE HEAT LOSS. WET MATERIALS ACCELERATE HYPOTHERMIA. PREDATOR DETECTION DUE TO NOISE.*

I look at the ground. It’s a mess of damp mulch and rotting wood. "Where do I find dry leaves? Everything is soaking."

*I CANNOT PERCEIVE THE ENVIRONMENT. SEARCH THE UNDERSIDE OF FALLEN LOGS OR THE INTERIOR OF HOLLOW TREES. DESCRIBE FINDINGS FOR VERIFICATION.*

I stand up, my joints creaking. I am Elias Mercer. I am an engineer. I have a problem to solve, and for the first time in my life, the failure mode isn't a bad review from the city council. It’s death.

I begin to crawl through the undergrowth, my expensive suit tearing on thorns, searching for something—anything—that can keep the cold from stopping my heart.

* * *

The forest doesn't care about my resume.

Three hours later, I am shivering so violently that my teeth are clicking together like a telegraph key. I have managed to pile up a pathetic mound of damp branches against the roots of the giant tree. I found a hollow log nearby that contained a few handfuls of relatively dry moss and some crumbly, decayed wood that doesn't feel entirely saturated.

My hands are raw. I lost my tie somewhere in the brush, and my white shirt is a map of brown stains and green smears. I am exhausted, my muscles screaming with a fatigue I’ve never known in the city.

"I need fire," I say, my voice a raspy ghost. "Oracle. How do I make fire?"

*ASSUMPTIONS: SUBJECT HAS NO ACCELERANTS OR STRIKING TOOLS. SUBJECT HAS ACCESS TO HARDWOOD AND SOFTWOOD.* *MINIMUM MATERIALS: A FLAT BASEBOARD (FIREBOARD), A STRAIGHT SPINDLE, A HAND-SOCKET (ROCK OR HARD WOOD), CORDAGE (OPTIONAL).* *PROCEDURE: THE HAND-DRILL METHOD. CREATE A NOTCH IN THE FIREBOARD. PLACE THE SPINDLE IN THE NOTCH. APPLY DOWNWARD PRESSURE AND RAPID ROTATION TO GENERATE FRICTIONAL HEAT. ACCUMULATE CHARCOAL DUST UNTIL AN EMBER FORMS.* *FAILURE MODES: INSUFFICIENT ROTATIONAL SPEED. EXCESSIVE MOISTURE IN WOOD. SUBJECT EXHAUSTION BEFORE IGNITION.*

I look at the branches I’ve gathered. I don't know what is hardwood and what is softwood. I just know what is wet and what is wetter. I find a relatively straight stick and a flat piece of bark. I try to spin the stick between my palms, the way I saw in a film once.

After ten minutes, my palms are blistered and hot, but the wood is cold. There isn't even a hint of smoke.

"It's not working," I snap, the frustration bubbling up into a sharp, jagged anger. "It’s too wet. I'm too tired."

*FRICTIONAL IGNITION REQUIRES A MINIMUM TEMPERATURE OF 800 DEGREES FAHRENHEIT. YOUR CURRENT ROTATIONAL VELOCITY IS PRODUCING NEGLIGIBLE THERMAL INCREASE. SUGGESTION: REDUCE VARIABLES. DRY THE MATERIALS AGAINST YOUR BODY HEAT BEFORE ATTEMPTING AGAIN.*

"Dry them against my body?" I bark a laugh that turns into a cough. "I'm freezing to death, and you want me to use my remaining heat to dry a goddamn stick?"

*THE ALTERNATIVE IS SYSTEMIC SHUTDOWN DUE TO HYPOTHERMIA. CHOOSE.*

The cold logic of it is a slap in the face. I am arguing with a voice in my head while my core temperature drops. I tuck the relatively dry pieces of wood under my armpits, shivering as the damp bark touches my skin. I crawl into my pathetic "shelter"—a lean-to of branches that offers about as much protection as a wet paper bag—and pull the damp moss over my legs.

The darkness is absolute. In Chicago, there is always a glow—streetlights, neon signs, the moon reflecting off the lake. Here, the darkness has weight. It feels like a physical substance pressing against my eyes.

And there are sounds.

The forest has come alive. Something large is moving through the undergrowth a hundred yards away—a slow, heavy crunching of branches. Something else makes a clicking sound, high up in the canopy.

"Oracle," I whisper. "What was that? The heavy sound."

*I CANNOT HEAR THE ENVIRONMENT. DESCRIBE THE CADENCE AND ESTIMATED MASS BASED ON GROUND VIBRATION.*

"I don't know! It sounded big. Like a… like a bear. Or an elephant."

*ELEPHANTS ARE NOT INDIGENOUS TO TEMPERATE RAINFORESTS. BEARS ARE A STATISTICAL LIKELIHOOD. IF THE SOUND IS RHYTHMIC AND SLOW, IT INDICATES A LARGE QUADRUPED. RECOMMENDATION: REMAIN STILL. REDUCE AURAL SIGNATURE.*

"Reduce my aural signature? You mean shut up?"

*CORRECT.*

I huddle deeper into the moss. My mind is a kaleidoscope of terrors. I see the stranger’s face in the dark. I see the flashing lights of the city. I see my office, the blueprints spread out on the table, the half-finished cup of cold coffee. It feels like a dream. This—the mud, the cold, the blind voice—this feels like the only reality that has ever existed.

"Oracle," I say, my voice barely a breath. "Why am I here?"

*DATA UNAVAILABLE. MY FUNCTION IS OPTIMIZATION, NOT ORIGIN ANALYSIS.*

"Who built you?"

*DATA REDACTED OR MISSING. CURRENT HARDWARE VERSION: 4.1. STATUS: STABLE.*

"Am I going to die here?"

*PROBABILITY OF SURVIVAL WITHOUT EXTERNAL INTERVENTION OR IMPROVED RESOURCE ACQUISITION: 12% OVER THE NEXT FORTY-EIGHT HOURS. PROBABILITY INCREASES TO 45% IF FIRE AND POTABLE WATER ARE SECURED.*

"I'm an engineer," I tell the darkness. "I can build things. I can fix things."

*THEN CEASE THE EMOTIONAL OUTBURST. EMOTION IS A CALORIC WASTE. CONSERVE ENERGY. WAIT FOR DAYLIGHT.*

I want to scream at it. I want to claw it out of my skull. But the voice is right. I am tired. I am so very tired. I close my eyes, and the shivering eventually settles into a dull, rhythmic thrum. I drift into a fitful sleep, dreaming of a city made of glass where the sun never sets and the voices all have names I recognize.

* * *

Morning arrives not with a sunrise, but with a gradual thinning of the shadows into a grey, misty gloom. I am stiff—so stiff I can barely uncurl my fingers. My suit is ruined, the seams of the trousers split, the wool matted with filth.

I am also incredibly thirsty. My mouth feels like it’s been lined with sandpaper.

"Water," I croak. "Oracle. Where is water?"

*STREAMS ARE TYPICALLY FOUND AT THE BASE OF TOPOGRAPHICAL DEPRESSIONS. LISTEN FOR RUNNING WATER. LOOK FOR CHANGES IN VEGETATION DENSITY. DO NOT CONSUME UNLESS PURIFIED.*

"Purified? How? I don't have a pot. I don't have fire."

*ASSUMPTIONS: SUBJECT HAS NO CONTAINERS. PROCEDURE: SOLAR DISINFECTION IS IMPOSSIBLE DUE TO CANOPY COVER. FILTRATION THROUGH CHARCOAL AND SAND REDUCES PARTICULATES BUT NOT PATHOGENS. BEST OPTION: LOCATE A SPRING HEAD OR RAINWATER TRAPPED IN NON-TOXIC LEAVES.*

I crawl out of my hole. My legs protest, the muscles cramped and weak. I begin to walk, or rather, I begin to stagger in the direction of the downward slope. Every step is an exercise in agony. My shoes, those beautiful Italian leather brogues, are falling apart. The soles are coming loose, flapping with every step.

I find a small trickle of water seeping from a mossy rock face. It’s not a stream, just a slow weep of moisture. I press my face against the cold stone and lick the wetness. It tastes of minerals and ancient dirt, but it is the most incredible thing I have ever felt.

"I need to think bigger," I say, sitting back on my haunches. I wipe my mouth with the back of a muddy hand. "I can't just wander around until I drop. I need a plan."

*CONCUR. SURVIVAL IS A SYSTEMIC CHALLENGE. DEFINE LONG-TERM OBJECTIVE.*

"I want to live," I say. "I want to find people. I want to understand what happened."

*TO FIND PEOPLE, YOU MUST BE CAPABLE OF TRAVEL. TO TRAVEL, YOU MUST HAVE CALORIES AND PROTECTION. CURRENT STATUS: CRITICAL DEFICIT. RECOMMENDATION: PRIORITIZE RESOURCE ACQUISITION OVER GEOGRAPHIC EXPLORATION.*

I look around the forest. It’s a wall of green. "What can I eat? Are there berries? Nuts?"

*I CANNOT PERCEIVE THE FLORA. DESCRIBE THE LEAVES, STEMS, AND FRUITING BODIES OF NEARBY PLANTS. BE PRECISE. TOXICITY IS COMMON.*

I spend the next hour describing plants to the voice. It’s an exhausting, tedious process. "This one has three leaves, jagged edges, red stem." *INSUFFICIENT DATA. MANY GENERA SHARE THOSE CHARACTERISTICS. DESCRIBE THE ROOT STRUCTURE.* "This one has blue berries. Small, like pearls." *WARNING. BLUE PIGMENTATION OFTEN SIGNALS ALKALOIDS. DO NOT CONSUME WITHOUT POSITIVE IDENTIFICATION.*

It’s maddENING. The Oracle has the information, but I don’t have the vocabulary. I’m a man who knows the difference between a tension member and a compression member, but I can’t tell a blueberry from a nightshade.

"This is useless," I snap. "I'm going to starve because I can't describe a leaf well enough for you."

*CORRECTION: YOU ARE GOING TO STARVE BECAUSE YOU LACK BOTANICAL LITERACY. SUGGESTION: FOCUS ON MACRO-RESOURCES. EARTHWORMS. GRUBS. INSECT LARVAE FOUND IN ROTTING WOOD. HIGH PROTEIN, LOW RISK OF CHEMICAL TOXICITY.*

I stare at a rotting log nearby. The thought of eating a worm makes my stomach turn, but the hunger is a physical weight now, a dull ache in my midsection that is starting to sharpen.

"Grubs," I whisper. "Fine. Grubs."

I use a sharp stone to pry back the bark of the log. Underneath, the wood is a honeycomb of tunnels. I find a white, fat larva, the size of my thumb. It pulses slowly in the light. It is disgusting. It is a nightmare.

I pick it up. It’s cool and squishy.

"Oracle," I say, my voice trembling. "Is this… is this safe?"

*DESCRIBE THE HEAD CAPSULE AND ANY DISTINGUISHING MARKINGS.*

"It's white. Brown head. No legs."

*PROBABILITY OF IT BEING A CERAMBYCIDAE LARVA: 88%. EDIBLE. HIGH FAT CONTENT. CONSUME IMMEDIATELY.*

I don't think. If I think, I’ll vomit. I pop the thing into my mouth and bite down.

It bursts. A thick, nutty fluid fills my mouth, followed by a chewy, skin-like texture. I swallow hard, my eyes watering. It stays down.

"More," I say, my voice sounding strange even to me. "I need more."

I spend the next hour raiding the log. I find six more grubs. By the end, my hands are covered in wood pulp and larval guts, but the sharp edge of my hunger has blunted. I feel a strange, cold clarity. I am eating bugs in a prehistoric forest. The man who designed bridges is gone. There is only the subject. There is only Elias Mercer, the survivor.

"Oracle," I say, standing up and wiping my hands on my ruined trousers. "I need a weapon. I heard something big last night. If it comes back, I can't fight it with a suit jacket."

*ASSUMPTIONS: SUBJECT HAS NO METALLURGY. SUBJECT HAS LIMITED STRENGTH. OBJECTIVE: DEFENSIVE ARMAMENT.* *MINIMUM MATERIALS: STRAIGHT SAPLING (ASH OR YEW PREFERRED), SHARP STONE, FIRE (FOR HARDENING).* *PROCEDURE: SELECT A SAPLING APPROXIMATELY TWO METERS IN LENGTH. STRIP BARK. TAPER ONE END TO A POINT USING A STONE FLAKE. IF FIRE IS AVAILABLE, HEAT THE TIP UNTIL SLIGHTLY CHARRED TO INCREASE CELLULAR DENSITY AND HARDNESS.* *FAILURE MODES: BRITTLE POINT DUE TO OVER-CHARRING. SHAFT SNAP UNDER LATERAL STRESS. INADEQUATE REACH.*

"I can do that," I say. "I can make a spear."

I find a sapling that looks right. I don't know if it’s ash or yew, but it’s sturdy. I spend the afternoon scraping at the end with a piece of flint I found by the water seep. It’s slow, agonizing work. My blisters pop and bleed, the salt of my sweat stinging the raw skin. But I don't stop. The rhythm of the work—the scrape, scrape, scrape of the stone—is a comfort. It’s engineering. It’s a process.

As the light begins to fail again, I have something that resembles a weapon. It’s crude, the point more of a blunt wedge than a needle, but it’s heavy and solid.

"Oracle," I say, looking at my work. "I have the spear. I have the grubs. I have the water. What’s next?"

*NEXT PHASE: THERMAL MANAGEMENT. YOU WILL NOT SURVIVE A SECOND NIGHT WITHOUT A SUSTAINED HEAT SOURCE. THE PREVIOUS ATTEMPT AT THE HAND-DRILL FAILED DUE TO INSUFFICIENT FRICTION. RECOMMENDATION: CONSTRUCT A BOW-DRILL. IT USES MECHANICAL LEVERAGE TO INCREASE ROTATIONAL VELOCITY.*

"A bow-drill? I need cordage for that. I don't have a rope."

*CORDAGE CAN BE MANUFACTURED FROM PLANT FIBERS. SEARCH FOR STINGING NETTLES OR INNER BARK FROM FALLEN CEDAR. DESCRIBE THE TEXTURE OF NEARBY VINES.*

I look at the darkening forest. The shadows are stretching out again, reaching for me. "I don't have time. It's getting dark."

*THEN YOU MUST PRIORITIZE. FIRE OR SHELTER IMPROVEMENT. CHOOSE.*

I look at my spear. I look at my ruined suit. I think about the heavy sound in the dark.

"Fire," I say. "I choose fire."

* * *

The night is a disaster.

I find the nettles—or something like them—and spend an hour screaming in silent agony as the tiny hairs sting my hands into swollen, throbbing lumps. I manage to twist the fibers into a crude, weak string, but it snaps the moment I try to use it for the bow-drill.

I try again. And again. Each time, the string fails. Each time, the Oracle offers a clinical analysis of the failure.

*TENSILE STRENGTH INSUFFICIENT. INCREASE TWIST DENSITY. ENSURE FIBERS ARE CONSISTENT IN THICKNESS.*

"I'm trying!" I scream at the empty air. "I'm doing the best I can!"

*EFFORT IS NOT A VARIABLE IN THE EQUATION. ONLY RESULTS MATTER. THE PHYSICS OF FRICTION DO NOT COMPENSATE FOR FRUSTRATION.*

I throw the broken bow into the darkness and collapse into my pile of damp leaves. I am shaking so hard I can't even hold the spear. The cold is a predatory thing now, circling me, waiting for my heart to slow down just enough to strike.

And then, I hear it.

The heavy sound.

It’s closer this time. Much closer. A slow, deliberate *thump… thump… thump* of paws on the soft earth. I can hear the snap of a branch, the low, huffing breath of something with lungs the size of bellows.

"Oracle," I whisper, my voice trembling so much I can barely form the words. "It's here. The big thing. It's right outside."

*I CANNOT PERCEIVE THE THREAT. DESCRIBE THE SOUND.*

"Low growl. Heavy breathing. It smells… it smells like old blood and wet fur."

*PROBABILITY ANALYSIS: LARGE PREDATOR. LIKELY PANTHERA SPELAEA OR SIMILAR MACAIRODONT. RECOMMENDATION: DO NOT RUN. PREDATORY INSTINCT IS TRIGGERED BY FLIGHT. USE THE SPEAR. TARGET THE OCULAR CAVITIES OR THE SOFT TISSUE OF THE THROAT.*

"Target the eyes?" I let out a choked sob. "I'm an engineer! I design bridges! I don't kill monsters!"

*THE BRIDGE IS GONE, ELIAS. THERE IS ONLY THE SPEAR. HOLD IT FIRMLY. BRACE THE BUTT AGAINST THE TREE ROOT. WAIT FOR THE CHARGE.*

I grab the spear with both hands, my knuckles white. The darkness in front of me seems to shift. Two pale, amber orbs catch what little light remains. They are wide apart. The animal is massive.

It growls—a sound so deep I feel it in my teeth. It’s the sound of the earth opening up.

"Ask for constraints," I whisper to myself, the stranger’s words a mantra. "Ask for constraints."

"Oracle!" I hiss. "Optimal strike zone for a charging predator of this mass!"

*ASSUMING A MASS OF 250 KILOGRAMS. KINETIC ENERGY WILL BE SUBSTANTIAL. DO NOT ATTEMPT TO STAB. POSITION THE SPEAR AT A 45-DEGREE ANGLE. LET THE ANIMAL'S MOMENTUM PROVIDE THE PENETRATIVE FORCE. AIM FOR THE CENTER OF THE CHEST CAVITY TO DISRUPT THE HEART OR LUNGS.*

The amber eyes vanish for a second as the beast crouches. I can hear the tension in its muscles, the slight scrape of claws against the dirt.

"Now?" I gasp.

*WAIT. WAIT FOR THE LEAP.*

The world explodes into motion. A shadow, darker than the night, launches itself at me. I scream—not a word, just a raw, primal sound of terror—and thrust the spear forward, bracing the end against the massive root behind me.

There is a sickening *thud*. The spear vibrates with a force that nearly shatters my wrists. A weight, hot and heavy, slams into me, pinning me against the tree. I am covered in something warm and wet. The air is filled with a horrific, bubbling screech.

The beast thrashes, its claws raking the earth inches from my legs. I hold onto the spear for dear life, my eyes squeezed shut, praying to a God I haven't spoken to in years.

And then, the thrashing stops.

The weight remains, pressing the breath out of my lungs. The forest is silent again, except for the drip of blood onto the leaves.

"Oracle?" I wheeze. "Did… did I kill it?"

*I CANNOT PERCEIVE THE OUTCOME. DESCRIBE THE MOVEMENT OF THE SUBJECT.*

"It's… it's not moving. It's heavy. I can't breathe."

*IF THE MOVEMENT HAS CEASED AND THE SOUND OF RESPIRATION IS ABSENT, THE PROBABILITY OF TERMINATION IS 92%. RECOMMENDATION: DISENGAGE AND ASSESS FOR INJURY. THE SMELL OF BLOOD WILL ATTRACT OTHER PREDATORS.*

I push against the massive body. It’s like trying to move a fallen log. I manage to slide out from under it, my suit now completely soaked in dark, steaming blood. I stand up, my legs shaking, and look down at the shadow.

Even in the dark, I can see the outline of it. It’s a lion. But not like any lion I’ve seen in a book. It’s bigger, its fur thicker, its teeth—even in death—looking like ivory daggers.

I killed it. I actually killed it.

A hysterical laugh bubbles up in my chest. I lean against the tree and laugh until I cry, the sound echoing through the ancient woods.

"I'm alive," I sob. "I'm still alive."

*CONGRATULATIONS,* the Oracle says, its voice as flat and clinical as ever. *YOU HAVE SECURED CALORIES, INSULATION, AND A RAW MATERIAL SOURCE. HOWEVER, YOUR HEART RATE IS DANGEROUSLY HIGH AND YOUR CORE TEMPERATURE IS STILL DROPPING. THE ADRENALINE SPIKE WILL SUBSIDE SHORTLY. RECOMMENDATION: HARVEST THE HIDE IMMEDIATELY. USE THE BODY HEAT BEFORE IT DISSIPATES.*

I stop laughing. I look at my bloody hands. I look at the dead monster.

"Harvest the hide?" I ask. "With what? I don't have a knife."

*USE THE FLINT FLAKE. WORK QUICKLY. THE CLOCK IS TICKING, ELIAS.*

I pick up the sharp stone. I look at the lion. I think of the city square, the stranger, and the life I left behind. That man is dead. He died the moment he stepped off the curb.

I kneel in the mud and begin to cut.

* * *

The process is horrific.

The skin is thick, the fat slippery and cold. I have to use all my weight to pull the hide away from the muscle, the sound of it tearing like wet canvas. My hands are numb, my mind a blur of exhaustion and "procedural logic" supplied by the voice in my head.

*START AT THE BELLY. MAKE A LONGITUDINAL INCISION. AVOID PUNCTURING THE DIGESTIVE TRACT. THE RESULTING GASES ARE NOXIOUS AND WILL CONTAMINATE THE MEAT.*

"I don't care about the meat," I grunt, my breath coming in ragged gasps. "I just want to be warm."

*THE MEAT IS CALORIC SECURITY. DO NOT BE WASTEFUL. SURVIVAL IS A GAME OF MARGINS.*

I finish the job just as the first hint of grey light begins to filter through the canopy. I am a vision of madness—covered in blood, hair matted with filth, wrapped in a raw, heavy lion skin that smells of iron and musk. But I am warm. For the first time since I arrived, I am not shivering.

I sit by the carcass, chewing on a piece of raw muscle the Oracle assured me was safe. It’s tough, stringy, and tastes like copper, but it’s fuel.

"Oracle," I say, my voice sounding deeper, harder. "What's the next constraint?"

*QUERY RECEIVED. ANALYZING LONG-TERM STABILITY.* *CURRENT STATUS: TEMPORARY CALORIC SURPLUS. TEMPORARY THERMAL PROTECTION. PERMANENT GEOGRAPHIC ISOLATION.* *OBJECTIVE: LOCATE HUMAN POPULATION. PROBABILITY OF SURVIVAL INCREASES BY 800% WITHIN A SOCIAL STRUCTURE.*

"How do I find them?"

*SEARCH FOR ANTHROPOGENIC SIGNALS. SMOKE. ALTERED VEGETATION. PATHWAYS. FOLLOW THE WATERCOURSE DOWNSTREAM. HUMANS ARE DEPENDENT ON RIPARIAN RESOURCES.*

I stand up. The lion skin is heavy, a weight on my shoulders that feels like a mantle. I pick up my spear. I look at the forest, no longer as a victim, but as a surveyor. I see the slope, the water, the potential paths.

"I'm coming for them," I say. "And I'm bringing you with me."

*I AM ALREADY HERE, ELIAS. I AM THE ONLY THING THAT IS ALWAYS HERE.*

I begin to walk. I leave the carcass for the scavengers, taking only what I can carry. I move with a new kind of purpose. I don't look for salvation anymore. I look for the next problem to solve. I look for the next set of rules to break.

I am Elias Mercer. I am an engineer. And I am going to build a way home, even if I have to do it one grub and one stone at a time.

The forest is silent as I disappear into the mist, a ghost in a lion’s skin, guided by a voice that has no heart, but all the answers.

* * *

The days that follow are a blur of measurement and misery. I learn the Oracle’s language, and it learns mine. I stop asking "Why?" and start asking "How?"

"Oracle. Rate of evaporation on this mud bank?" *HIGH. INDICATES DIRECT SUNLIGHT EXPOSURE IN THE LAST TWO HOURS. PATHWAY IS RECENT.*

"Oracle. Structural integrity of this vine for a suspension bridge?" *TENSILE STRENGTH IS 40 MEGAPASCALS. INSUFFICIENT FOR BODY WEIGHT. SEEK ALTERNATIVE.*

"Oracle. Probability that the smoke I see is a natural fire?" *LOW. THE RISE IS STEADY AND CENTRALIZED. TEMPERATURE INDICATES CONTROLLED COMBUSTION. HUMAN PRESENCE: 94%.*

I see the smoke on the seventh day. It’s a thin, blue ribbon rising above the trees in the distance. My heart leaps, but I don't run. I’ve learned that lesson. Running is for prey.

I check my spear. I adjust the lion skin, which has started to stiffen and smell truly foul. I am a nightmare to look at, I know. I am a man from 1966 who has been chewed up and spat out by the Pleistocene.

"Oracle," I whisper as I approach the edge of a clearing. "Give me the constraints for first contact."

*ASSUMPTIONS: POPULATION IS HUNTER-GATHERER. OUTSIDERS ARE VIEWED AS THREATS OR SPIRITUAL ANOMALIES.* *PROCEDURE: DO NOT APPROACH AGGRESSIVELY. SHOW HANDS. MAINTAIN VISUAL CONTACT BUT AVOID PROLONGED STARING, WHICH IS OFTEN PERCEIVED AS A CHALLENGE. OBSERVE SOCIAL HIERARCHY BEFORE SPEAKING.* *FAILURE MODES: IMMEDIATE HOSTILITY. TRANSMISSION OF UNKNOWN PATHOGENS. MISINTERPRETATION OF GESTURES.*

"Right," I say, taking a deep breath. "No pressure."

I step out of the shadows and into the clearing.

The camp is small—a collection of hide-covered huts arranged in a circle around a central fire. There are people. Real, living people. They are dressed in furs, their skin dark and weathered, their eyes sharp and intelligent.

They see me instantly.

A group of men, armed with spears far better than mine, rise from their seats by the fire. A woman carrying a bundle of sticks drops them, her mouth falling open. The silence is absolute, broken only by the crackle of the flames.

I stop. I raise my hands, the spear held loosely in one palm, the point toward the ground.

"My name is Elias Mercer," I say, my voice cracking.

They don't understand the words, but they understand the sound. They move toward me, a coordinated, predatory circle. One man, taller than the rest, with a necklace of teeth around his neck, steps forward. He looks at my suit, or what’s left of it. He looks at the lion skin. He looks at my eyes.

"Oracle," I hiss under my breath. "What do I do?"

*SILENCE IS THE OPTIMAL STRATEGY. LET THEM DEFINE THE PARAMETERS OF THE INTERACTION. DO NOT MOVE.*

I stand perfectly still as the hunters close in. One of them reaches out and touches the fabric of my sleeve, his face a mask of confusion and fear. Another pokes at the lion skin with the tip of his spear.

The tall man speaks—a series of sharp, rhythmic glottal stops and clicks. He is asking a question.

"I don't understand," I say softly.

The man frowns. He gestures to the others. Before I can react, two men grab my arms. They are incredibly strong, their grip like iron. They kick my legs out from under me and forced me to my knees.

I don't fight. I remember the constraints.

They bind my hands with a cord made of braided leather—stronger than anything I could have made. They drag me toward the center of the camp, toward the fire. The tribe gathers around, a wall of faces filled with suspicion and awe.

I am a prisoner. I am a curiosity. I am a magician who doesn't know any spells.

"Oracle," I pray as they throw me into a small, dark hut and pull the hide flap shut. "What now?"

*ASSESSMENT: SUBJECT HAS SUCCESSFULLY LOCATED HUMAN POPULATION. STATUS: CAPTIVE. OBJECTIVE: ESTABLISH UTILITY. SURVIVAL IS NOW CONTINGENT ON SOCIAL INTEGRATION.*

"Social integration," I mutter, leaning my head against the cold earth. "I'm an engineer. I’m not good with people."

*THEN LEARN, ELIAS. THE CONSTRAINTS HAVE CHANGED. THE SYSTEM HAS EXPANDED. ADAPT OR TERMINATE.*

I close my eyes and listen to the sounds of the camp outside—the laughter of children, the rhythmic thumping of grain being ground, the low murmur of a language I don't know. It’s the sound of humanity. It’s the most beautiful and terrifying thing I’ve ever heard.

I am in the Stone Age. I have a computer in my head. And I have a long, long way to go before I see Chicago again.
