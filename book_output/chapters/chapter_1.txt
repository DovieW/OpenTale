SCENE:

The humidity in Chicago usually tastes like exhaust and old concrete, but today, June 14, 1966, it carries a strange, metallic tang. I check my watch. 12:14 PM. The midday rush in the square is a frantic machine, a sea of narrow ties, summer dresses, and the relentless rhythmic clicking of heels against the pavement. I am a part of it, just another structural engineer with a briefcase full of blueprints and a mind occupied by load-bearing capacities and the rising cost of steel.

The crowd is a blur of motion, but he is a static point.

He stands directly in my path, ten feet away. He doesn’t weave. He doesn’t look for an opening. He simply waits. He wears a charcoal suit that looks too heavy for the heat, yet he isn’t sweating. His face is unremarkable—the kind of face that belongs on a man who sells life insurance or fixes clocks—but his eyes are locked onto mine with a terrifying, singular focus.

I try to sidestep him. He moves with me, a mirror image.

"Elias Mercer," he says.

My name sounds wrong coming from him. It sounds like a fact being checked off a list. I stop, my briefcase bumping against my knee. "Do I know you?"

The man doesn't answer the question. He steps closer, leaning in until I can smell nothing—no cologne, no breath, just that ozone-heavy metallic scent. He speaks with the flat cadence of someone reciting a law.

"When the voice arrives, don’t ask for salvation," he says. His voice is low, cutting through the roar of city buses and distant sirens. "Ask for constraints."

"What? Look, I’m in a hurry—"

"Ask for constraints, Elias."

Then, the world breaks.

It starts with the sound. The roar of the square doesn't fade; it vanishes. One moment, a woman is laughing three feet away; the next, her mouth is moving in a vacuum. The colors of the city—the yellow taxis, the red brick—bleed into a muddy, vibrating grey. The edges of the buildings blur, stretching upward like pulled taffy. 

A high-pitched whine pierces my skull, a needle of pure silver driven into my ear canal. I reach for my head, but my hands aren't there. I can't feel the weight of the briefcase. I can't feel the pavement under my shoes. 

The stranger is the last thing I see. He doesn't move. He doesn't blink. He simply watches as the grey devours him, devours the square, devours me.

I collapse into a void that has no bottom.

***

Air.

Cold, wet air hits my lungs, and I realize I’m screaming. The sound is raw and jagged, tearing at a throat that feels like it’s been scoured with sand. I choke, my mouth filling with the taste of damp earth and rotting leaves.

I roll onto my side, gasping. My fingers dig into something soft and freezing. Not pavement. Not linoleum. Mud. Deep, black, ancient mud.

I lie there for a long time, my heart hammering a frantic rhythm against my ribs. My vision is a kaleidoscope of greens and browns, slowly coalescing into shapes. Massive, gnarled trunks rise like the pillars of a cathedral gone mad. The canopy above is so dense it swallows the sun, allowing only a few sickly shafts of grey light to pierce the gloom. The air is heavy, laden with the scent of pine resin, damp fur, and something primordial—a smell of growth and decay so intense it makes my stomach turn.

I am overdressed. I realize this with a hysterical sort of clarity. I am wearing a bespoke wool suit, a silk tie, and polished oxfords in the middle of a forest that looks like it hasn't seen a human being since the glaciers retreated.

"Where..." My voice is a pathetic croak. 

I try to sit up. My head spins, the world tilting at a sickening angle. I’m thirsty. Not just thirsty—parched. My tongue feels like a piece of dry leather stuck to the roof of my mouth. Dehydration. I know the signs. I’ve spent enough time on sun-baked construction sites to recognize the early onset of a shutdown.

I need to get up. I need to find a landmark. A road. A power line. Anything.

I manage to push myself to my knees. The forest is silent, save for the distant, rhythmic thrumming of a bird I don't recognize and the rustle of wind through leaves the size of dinner plates. It’s too big. The scale is all wrong. These trees—oaks, maybe, or some ancestor of them—are hundreds of feet tall, their roots twisting across the ground like the coils of sleeping giants.

*Reality stuttered,* I think, my mind grasping for a rational explanation. *A stroke? A seizure? This is a hallucination. A very, very cold hallucination.*

A drop of rain hits my neck, sliding down inside my collar. It’s ice-cold. If this is a dream, my subconscious has an incredible grasp of thermodynamics.

*INPUT PARAMETERS REQUIRED.*

I freeze. The voice isn't coming from the woods. It isn't coming from the air. It’s inside my head. It’s flat, vibrating against the base of my skull, devoid of inflection or gender. It sounds like a computer, but more immediate, more invasive.

"Who's there?" I spin around, nearly falling back into the mud. "Who said that?"

*SYSTEM ACTIVE. STANDBY FOR INITIALIZATION. DEFINE OBJECTIVE.*

I squeeze my eyes shut, pressing my palms against my temples. "I'm losing my mind. I'm in a coma. That's it. I'm in a hospital in Chicago, and this is the morphine talking."

*OBJECTIVE NOT DEFINED. STANDBY.*

The stranger’s face flashes in my mind. The grey suit. The ozone smell. *When the voice arrives, don’t ask for salvation. Ask for constraints.*

My breath is coming in short, ragged hitches. "Are you... the voice?"

*I AM PROJECT ORACLE. I REQUIRE INPUT PARAMETERS TO OPTIMIZE OUTPUT.*

It isn't a hallucination. It’s too consistent, too clinical. And it isn't an oracle in the mystical sense. It’s an engine. A machine sitting in my brain, waiting for a command.

"Help me," I whisper. "Get me out of here. Save me."

*INSUFFICIENT DATA. 'HELP' IS NOT A PARAMETER. 'SAVE' IS NOT A QUANTIFIABLE OBJECTIVE. DEFINE CONSTRAINTS AND DESIRED OUTCOME.*

A bitter laugh bubbles up in my throat. It isn't a rescuer. It’s a calculator. It’s a tool, and right now, I’m too panicked to use it.

*Panic burns daylight.*

The thought hits me with the force of a physical blow. It’s a rule from somewhere deep in my training—or maybe just common sense. The sun, what little I can see of it through the canopy, is moving. The shadows are stretching. If I spend the next hour screaming at the sky, I’ll be screaming in the dark. And in a forest this size, the dark is where things end.

"Inventory," I mutter, forced into a grim focus. "Do an inventory, Elias."

I sit back on my heels and begin to strip. Not everything, just enough to see what I have.

Item one: The suit. High-quality wool, two-piece. It’s heavy, which is good for warmth, but once it gets soaked, it’ll stay wet for days. It’ll weigh me down and suck the heat out of my core. 
Item two: The shirt. Cotton. Useless for insulation once wet. 
Item three: The tie. Silk. A potential cord, though a weak one.
Item four: The shoes. Leather oxfords. Thin soles. No traction. Already ruined by the mud.
Item five: The watch. My grandfather’s Omega. Mechanical. It’s still ticking. 12:48 PM.
Item six: My pockets. A leather wallet containing forty-two dollars and a picture of my mother. A fountain pen. A handkerchief, clean and pressed. A pack of Lucky Strikes. A Zippo lighter.

Item seven: The briefcase.

It’s half-swallowed by the mud a few feet away. I drag it free by the handle and the leather comes up slick and cold in my palm.

Blueprints. A slide rule. A little grey calculator with stiff keys. A steel straightedge.

I flip through the pages like I’m checking for a hidden door back to Chicago.

There isn’t one.

I shut it and set it on a root, out of the worst of the wet. It feels like abandoning a part of myself. It also feels like a weight I can’t carry.

I grab the Zippo like it’s a religious relic. I flick the wheel. 

Nothing. Just a spark.

I shake it. It’s light. Bone dry. I must have forgotten to fill it this morning. I want to throw it against a tree, to watch it shatter, but I force my hand to stay still. A metal casing is still a tool. A spark is still a spark.

"Oracle," I say, my voice steadier now. "I am in a forest. I am dehydrated. I am wearing modern clothing. I have no food, no functional lighter, and no shelter. What is my status?"

*ASSUMPTION: SUBJECT IS IN A TEMPERATE PRIMEVAL FOREST ENVIRONMENT. 
CURRENT VITALS: ELEVATED CORTISOL, MILD DEHYDRATION, SHIVER RESPONSE INITIATING. 
IMMEDIATE THREATS: EXPOSURE (HYPOTHERMIA RISK UPON SUNSET), DEHYDRATION (CRITICAL WITHIN 24-48 HOURS), APEX PREDATORS.
RECOMMENDATION: ESTABLISH WATER SOURCE AND SHELTER PRIOR TO LIGHT LOSS.*

"Where is the water?"

*I HAVE NO EXTERNAL PERCEPTION. I CANNOT SEE, HEAR, OR SMELL YOUR ENVIRONMENT. PROVIDE OBSERVATIONS.*

I blink, confused. "You're in my head. You can't see what I see?"

*CORRECT. I OPERATE ON DATA PROVIDED BY THE SUBJECT. DESCRIBE TERRAIN, VEGETATION, AND AUDITORY INPUTS.*

I look around, trying to see like an engineer instead of a victim. "The ground slopes downward to my left. The mud is thicker there. The trees are larger. I hear a consistent... a low-frequency rushing sound. Distant. Maybe three hundred yards in the direction of the slope."

*PROBABILITY OF SURFACE WATER IN DESCRIBED DIRECTION: 84%. WATER FLOWS DOWNHILL. VEGETATION DENSITY SUGGESTS RIPARIAN ZONE.*

I start moving. Every step is a battle. The undergrowth is a tangle of ferns and thorny vines that snag on my trousers, tearing the expensive wool. My shoes slip on the wet roots, sending me sprawling more than once. My hands are soon covered in small, stinging cuts. 

The first time I think I've found water, it’s a shallow depression filled with rainwater and dead leaves.

It looks like tea. It smells like compost.

My mouth waters anyway.

"Oracle," I say, forcing the question into something usable, "stagnant water in a leaf basin. Risks?"

*DESCRIBE COLOR, ODOR, INSECT ACTIVITY, AND WHETHER THE WATER IS MOVING.*

"Dark brown. Still. Smells rotten. I see tiny worms." I swallow. "No fish. No current."

*PROBABILITY OF HIGH PATHOGEN LOAD: INCREASED. RECOMMENDATION: SEEK FLOWING WATER OR CREATE A SEEP WELL ADJACENT TO A FLOW SOURCE.*

"So, not this." I back away before my desperation makes the decision for me.

The second time, I follow the rushing sound and get turned around by the forest’s scale. Everything looks the same at ground level—roots, ferns, trunks thick as freight cars. The rushing fades, then returns, then fades again like it’s playing games.

I stop with my hands on my knees and breathe until the dizziness clears.

"Constraint," I whisper. "I can’t keep a straight line." I glance up at the canopy. "No sun. No landmarks."

*PROCEDURE: SELECT A DISTANT VISUAL REFERENCE POINT IN THE DIRECTION OF TRAVEL. MOVE TO IT. REPEAT. IF DISTANT POINTS ARE UNAVAILABLE, MARK YOUR PATH WITH HIGH-CONTRAST OBJECTS AT REGULAR INTERVALS.*

I tear a strip from the inside seam of my trouser leg and tie it to a low branch. White cloth against green.

"Two-week salary," I mutter.

*ALSO: EXTENDED SURVIVAL TIME.*

I pick a dead tree with a forked top like a scratch mark against the canopy and walk to it. Then another. Then another.

The rushing sound grows louder. It’s not a river; it’s a creek, swollen with recent rain. I break through a final curtain of massive ferns and see it—a ribbon of grey-brown water churning over mossy rocks.

I stumble toward it, falling to my knees at the bank. I reach out to scoop a handful to my lips.

"Oracle," I say, a sudden thought stopping me. "Is the water safe?"

*I CANNOT ANALYZE MICROBIAL CONTENT WITHOUT CHEMICAL SENSORS. HOWEVER, IN THIS TIME PERIOD, ANTHROPOGENIC POLLUTION IS ZERO. BIOLOGICAL CONTAMINANTS (PARASITES, BACTERIA) ARE HIGHLY PROBABLE. CONSUMPTION MAY LEAD TO DYSENTERY OR PARASITIC INFECTION. FAILURE MODE: SYSTEMIC DEHYDRATION VIA DIARRHEA.*

I stare at the water. My throat is a burning ache. "So I shouldn't drink it?"

*I DID NOT ADVISE AGAINST DRINKING. I STATED RISKS. WITHOUT PURIFICATION, YOU MUST WEIGH THE CERTAINTY OF DEHYDRATION AGAINST THE PROBABILITY OF INFECTION.*

"How do I purify it?"

*BOIL THE WATER. MINIMUM TEMPERATURE: 212 DEGREES FAHRENHEIT FOR ONE MINUTE.*

"I don't have a pot. I don't have a fire," I snap.

*THEN THE WATER IS NOT PURIFIED.*

I growl in frustration. The Oracle is technically correct and practically useless. It’s like talking to a textbook that won't turn its own pages.

I look at the creek. The water is murky with silt. I remember something from a Boy Scout manual thirty years ago. I move a few feet away from the bank and start digging into the sandy soil with my bare hands. The dirt is cold and packed tight. I break two fingernails before I manage to hollow out a hole about a foot deep.

Slowly, water begins to seep through the sand into my pit. It’s clearer than the creek, filtered by the earth.

"Oracle," I say, "I have dug a seep-well. Does this filter out the biological contaminants?"

*SAND FILTRATION REMOVES MACROSCOPIC DEBRIS AND SOME LARGE PARASITES. IT DOES NOT ELIMINATE MICROSCOPIC PATHOGENS OR VIRUSES. RISK REDUCTION: 15-20%.*

"Better than nothing," I mutter. I wait for the hole to fill, then cup my hands and drink. The water is freezing, metallic, and tastes like ancient stones. I drink until my stomach aches, then I splash my face, trying to wash away the salt of my tears and the grime of the city.

I drink slower than my body wants.

Each swallow is a negotiation.

"Oracle," I say, lowering my hands, "I need a decision rule. Given no fire and no container, when is it optimal to drink unpurified water?"

*RULE: IF SUBJECTIVE THIRST INCLUDES DIZZINESS, CONFUSION, OR DARK URINE, DRINK IMMEDIATELY. AVOID STAGNANT SOURCES. PREFER FLOWING OR FILTERED SOURCES. MINIMIZE VOLUME PER INTAKE TO REDUCE GI SHOCK. ACCEPT RISK.*

"Accept risk," I repeat. The words taste like rust.

I sit back, wiped out. My watch says 2:15 PM. The light is already failing, the shadows of the massive trees stretching across the creek like bars of a cage. 

"I need fire," I say. "I have a Zippo with no fluid. I have a silk tie and a cotton shirt. How do I make fire?"

*MULTIPLE METHODS AVAILABLE. GIVEN CURRENT MATERIALS: 1. CHEMICAL IGNITION (NOT POSSIBLE). 2. FRICTION. 3. PERCUSSION. 
RECOMMENDATION: PERCUSSION. YOU POSSESS A LIGHTER WITH A FLINT WHEEL. FLINT PRODUCES SPARKS AT APPROXIMATELY 3000 DEGREES FAHRENHEIT. 
REQUIREMENT: DRY TINDER.*

I look around. The world is a damp sponge. Everything is dripping. "There is no dry tinder. Everything is wet."

*SEARCH THE UNDERSIDE OF LEANING TREES OR THE INTERIOR OF ROTTING LOGS FOR DRY WOOD DUST. ALTERNATIVELY, USE THE DRY COMPONENTS OF YOUR CLOTHING.*

"Constraint," I say, flexing my fingers. They feel thick and clumsy. "Fine motor control is degrading. My hands are going numb." 

*RECOMMENDATION: PRIORITIZE TASKS BY DEXTERITY REQUIREMENT. COMPLETE FINE-MOTOR TASKS FIRST. GROSS-MOTOR TASKS (SHELTER, WOOD GATHERING) LATER.*

I look at my shirt. It’s damp with sweat and humid air. I look at my handkerchief. It’s in my pocket, relatively dry. I pull it out. It’s fine Irish linen. 

"If I use this, I won't have it for anything else."

*CORRECT. CHOOSE YOUR CONSTRAINTS.*

I find a fallen log that looks like it’s been dead for a century. I kick at the bark, tearing away chunks of soggy moss. Inside, the heartwood is crumbly. I scrape out a handful of the driest dust I can find and pile it on a flat stone. I shred a small corner of my handkerchief and place it on top.

I hold the Zippo over the pile. My hands are shaking. 

"Steady," I whisper. "Just like a bridge. One piece at a time."

I flick the wheel. *Scritch.* A spray of orange sparks rains down on the linen. Nothing happens.
*Scritch.*
*Scritch.*

A tiny black hole appears in the fabric. A microscopic wisp of smoke rises. I lean in, my heart stopping, and blow gently.

The spark dies.

"Why did it die?" I demand.

*ASSUMPTION: AIRFLOW WAS TOO FORCEFUL OR TINDER WAS INSUFFICIENTLY PREPARED. FRICTION FIRE REQUIRES A GRADIENT OF COMBUSTIBLES. YOU ATTEMPTED TO IGNITE A LARGE MASS WITH A MOMENTARY HEAT SOURCE.*

"I'm an engineer, for God's sake," I hiss. "I know how combustion works."

*KNOWLEDGE OF COMBUSTION DOES NOT CONJURE MATERIALS OR STEADY HANDS. SUCCESS REQUIRES PHYSICAL EXECUTION WITHIN ENVIRONMENTAL CONSTRAINTS.*

I try again. And again. I rip more of the handkerchief. I scrape more wood dust. By the time I stop, my thumb is raw and bleeding from the flint wheel, the handkerchief is a tattered rag, and the stone is covered in cold, black char. 

I force myself to stop because I can feel the wheel grinding down. The Zippo is not infinite. My thumb isn't, either.

I stare at my hands.

Back home, my calluses are from pencils.

Here, they’re going to be from surviving.

The sun is gone. The forest isn't grey anymore; it’s purple, turning to a deep, suffocating black. The temperature is dropping fast. I can see my breath now, little ghostly puffs in the dim light. My wool suit is damp, and a shiver starts in my lower back, radiating outward.

"I'm going to freeze," I say. The dry humor I usually use to deflect stress has evaporated, replaced by a cold, hard knot of fear.

*HYPOTHERMIA IS THE PRIMARY THREAT. WITHOUT FIRE, INSULATION IS MANDATORY. SHELTER CONSTRUCTION MUST BEGIN IMMEDIATELY.*

"I don't have time for a house, Oracle!"

*I DID NOT SUGGEST A HOUSE. I SUGGESTED SHELTER. MINIMUM VIABLE STRUCTURE: DEBRIS HUT. PURPOSE: TRAP BODY HEAT.*

I move with a frantic, clumsy energy. I find a low-hanging branch on a massive oak and start piling fallen limbs against it. I don't have a knife, so I have to break branches by wedging them into root crotches and leaning my entire weight against them. The cracks sound like gunshots in the quiet woods.

I pile on ferns, armloads of rotting leaves, and thick slabs of moss I peel off the rocks. I’m working in near-total darkness now, relying on touch. The forest is no longer silent. 

Somewhere in the distance, something snaps a heavy branch. It isn't the wind. It’s a deliberate, heavy weight.

I freeze, my hands buried in a pile of damp leaves. I listen.

*Crunch.*

It’s closer. Maybe fifty yards away. I can hear the creature’s breath—a low, huffing sound that vibrates in the air. This isn't a wolf. It’s bigger. Much bigger.

*AUDITORY INPUT INDICATES LARGE ANIMAL MOVEMENT,* the Oracle says.

"I told you that!" I hiss. "What is it?"

*INSUFFICIENT DATA. BASED ON SOUND PROFILE AND ENVIRONMENT: PROBABILITY OF PANTHERA SPELAEA (CAVE LION) OR URSUS SPELAEUS (CAVE BEAR). BOTH ARE APEX PREDATORS. BOTH ARE ACTIVE IN LOW-LIGHT CONDITIONS.*

My blood turns to ice. Cave lions? Cave bears? I’m not just in a forest; I’m in the wrong millennium.

The huffing sound moves to my right. The creature is circling the clearing. It knows I’m here. It’s smelling the sweat, the wool, the fear.

I back away from my half-finished shelter, my heart hammering so hard I can hear it in my ears. I reach into my pocket and grab the Zippo. It’s useless, but it’s the only metal I have.

"Oracle," I whisper, my voice trembling. "The stranger said... he said don't ask for salvation. Ask for constraints."

*I AM READY.*

"Give me the constraints," I say, forcing the words out. "What will kill me first? What do I prioritize right now? Give me a hierarchy of survival for the next six hours."

The Oracle’s voice changes. It’s no longer just answering; it’s structuring.

*OBJECTIVE: SURVIVAL UNTIL DAWN.
CONSTRAINT 1: THERMAL REGULATION. AMBIENT TEMPERATURE IS 42 DEGREES AND DROPPING. MOISTURE IN CLOTHING WILL ACCELERATE HEAT LOSS. 
CONSTRAINT 2: PREDATION. SUBJECT IS CURRENTLY UNARMED AND EXPOSED. 
CONSTRAINT 3: PSYCHOLOGICAL STABILITY. PANIC INCREASES METABOLIC RATE AND DEGRADES DECISION-MAKING.*

*PRIORITIZATION:
1. FIRE. FIRE ADDRESSES BOTH THERMAL REGULATION AND PREDATION. MOST APEX PREDATORS IN THIS ERA HAVE A SYSTEMIC AVERSION TO OPEN FLAME AND SMOKE.
2. SHELTER IMPROVEMENT. IF FIRE FAILS, INSULATION MUST BE INCREASED BY 400% TO PREVENT STAGE TWO HYPOTHERMIA.*

I look at my tattered shelter. I look at the dark woods where the heavy breathing continues, slow and rhythmic.

"If I work on the shelter, I might stay warm, but the lion eats me," I mutter. "If I try for fire and fail, I freeze to death and the lion eats my corpse."

*CORRECT.*

"How do I make the fire work? Tell me exactly what I did wrong."

*YOU ATTEMPTED TO IGNITE RAW WOOD DUST. YOU LACKED A SECONDARY VOLATILE AGENT. ASSUMPTION: YOUR WALLET CONTAINS PAPER CURRENCY.*

I reach for my wallet. Forty-two dollars. "It’s paper. It’s mostly cotton and linen."

*IT IS DRY. IT HAS A HIGH SURFACE-AREA-TO-VOLUME RATIO WHEN SHREDDED. IT IS THE MOST FLAMMABLE OBJECT IN YOUR POSSESSION.*

I pull out a ten-dollar bill. Hamilton looks back at me, stoic and useless. I start to shred it, my fingers numb with cold. I make a nest of paper strips, mixing them with the finest scrapings of wood dust and the last of my handkerchief.

I kneel on the ground. The huffing sound is closer now. I can smell the beast—a thick, musky scent like a wet dog that hasn't been washed in years.

"Choose, Elias," I whisper. "Fire or the hut."

I look at the dark, at the unseen eyes watching me from the brush. I think about the stranger in the grey suit. I think about the rules of engineering. You don't build a bridge on a foundation of luck. You build it on the most stable element you have.

The most stable element I have is the spark.

"Fire," I say, my voice cracking. "I choose fire."

I grip the Zippo. I don't look at the woods. I don't look at the shadows. I focus entirely on the small pile of shredded money and wood dust. I bring the lighter down, inches from the nest.

"Oracle," I say, "Give me the procedure for the spark."

*PROCEDURE: POSITION WHEEL AT 45-DEGREE ANGLE. APPLY MAXIMUM DOWNWARD PRESSURE. EXECUTE SINGLE, RAPID ROTATION. UPON SPARK GENERATION, DO NOT BLOW. WAIT FOR SMOLDER TO REACH THE SECONDARY FUEL LAYER.*

I take a breath. I hold it.

*Scritch.*
